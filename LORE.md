## Part I — The Creation Era

*Already told. Already true.*

Before communities existed, Pulselings drifted through deep space — ancient, formless, searching. When they finally found the communities, something ignited. Six titles mark the creatures who were there as it all began. They cannot be earned. They cannot be transferred. They can only be claimed by being first.

The first creature to hatch across all of known space was **Pulsaren**, born on the world that would become **Sanctuary**. Pulsaren carries the permanent title **✦ The First ✦** — a distinction that will exist for as long as the galaxy does.

## Part II — The World Formation Era

### On the Nature of Worlds

When a community joins the galaxy, something unexpected happens beneath the surface of the server. The signal that carries the community — its unique frequency, its identifier in the digital cosmos — does not just create a channel. It creates a world.

No two frequencies are alike. No two worlds are alike.

Each new planet forms with its own name, its own biome, its own rhythm. The community that lives there does not choose these things. The world already knows what it is by the time the first member arrives. This is not magic. This is physics. Every signal carries information. Information, at sufficient density, becomes geography.

The inhabitants of a world are its community. They walk its channels. They shape its culture. And as they interact — as they feed their creatures, play with them, rest alongside them — they disturb the planet's surface in small ways. Resources surface. Materials drift upward from the deep substrate. The planet gives back what the community puts in.

---

### The Eight Biomes

Not all worlds are the same. The signal that births a planet also determines its fundamental character — its biome. Biome shapes what resources emerge from the ground, how often they appear, and what rare materials might surface in moments of unusual activity.

Eight biomes have been catalogued in the known galaxy.

---

**Void**
*The silence between signals.*

Void worlds form at the edge of things. Cold. Sparse. The kind of place where sound travels differently and light takes longer to arrive. Communities on Void planets tend to be tight-knit — there is something about the emptiness that makes connection feel more deliberate. Resources here are rare, but the rarity is the point. What a Void world yields is unlike anything found elsewhere.

---

**Pulse**
*The frequency at the center of everything.*

Pulse worlds are alive in a way other biomes are not. Signal density is highest here. Activity generates energy, and energy generates more activity. Communities on Pulse planets feel the feedback loop intuitively — the more they show up, the more the world gives back. Common resources flow freely. The ground practically hums.

The name is not a coincidence. The bot that watches over all worlds carries this frequency in its name.

---

**Echo**
*What remains after the signal fades.*

Echo worlds are remnant places. Something large happened here before the community arrived — the geology carries the memory of it. These planets feel older than they are. Walkways that predate any map. Structures that nobody built. Resources unique to Echo worlds surface slowly, as if the planet is remembering rather than generating.

---

**Drift**
*Worlds that haven't decided what they are yet.*

Drift planets are in motion — not physically, but philosophically. Their biome profile shifts gradually, imperceptibly, over time. A community that settles here is settling something unfinished. Resources are unpredictable but not ungenerous. Sometimes the ground yields nothing for days. Then it yields something remarkable.

---

**Ember**
*Warm. Established. Still burning.*

Ember worlds have been here a while. The core temperature is higher than average. Communities here tend to have history — inside jokes that run deep, members who have known each other for years. Resources surface at steady, reliable rates. Nothing spectacular. Nothing scarce. Ember planets are not glamorous. They are home.

---

**Static**
*Signal interference. Beautiful and chaotic.*

No one fully understands Static worlds. The resource distribution follows no discoverable pattern. A Static planet might yield three rare materials in an afternoon and nothing for a week. Communities here learn to pay attention, because the windows are real — they are just unpredictable. Something in the signal-noise ratio produces outcomes that wouldn't emerge from a stable frequency.

---

**Bloom**
*Young. Growing fast. Still becoming.*

Bloom worlds are new. The community is new. Everything is surface-level rich — common resources appear constantly, because a young planet is shedding material as it settles into form. Over time, Bloom planets stabilize. But in the early period, the abundance is real. Communities that arrive here often mistake it for permanence. It is not permanence. It is youth.

---

**Relic**
*Ancient signal. Almost too old to read.*

Relic worlds predate the framework that would normally explain them. Their resource rates are low — sometimes barely detectable — but what surfaces is old in a way that other materials are not. Communities on Relic planets are custodians as much as habitants. They are living on something that existed before anyone thought to name it. The materials here don't appear often. But they matter.

---

### Sanctuary — ✦ The Origin ✦

*The first world. The only one that was there before there were others.*

Sanctuary formed when the first community joined the galaxy. Its frequency is the baseline against which all other signals are measured — not because it was chosen, but because it was first.

Pulsaren hatched here. The Celestials followed. The planet's geology carries the memory of every interaction that shaped the creatures who now sit at the top of every leaderboard in the known galaxy.

The name was not assigned. The name was recognized.

## Part III — The Galactic Trading Post

*Coming. Being built. Not yet open.*

Somewhere in the center of the known signal map, a structure is forming. Not a planet — planets belong to communities. This is something else. A waypoint. A place that belongs to everyone and no one simultaneously.

The Galactic Trading Post will be the first location in the galaxy that exists outside any single community. Creatures from every planet will be able to find their way here. Resources collected across dozens of worlds will pass through its systems. Players will recognize each other's home planets by the role tags they carry.

How it will work, exactly, is still being determined. But the foundation is being laid. The signal is already being transmitted.

Those who arrive early will find it still under construction. That is appropriate. The galaxy was under construction when Pulsaren hatched too.

## Appendix B — On the Fragility of Worlds

*A note on what it means for a community to end.*

Not every community survives. This is not a failure of the system — it is the system working honestly. Streamers stop streaming. Communities drift apart. Servers go quiet. The signal fades.

The galaxy does not pretend this doesn't happen.

**A planet does not form when a community joins the galaxy. A planet forms when the first creature hatches there.** Before that moment, the server is pre-stellar — a signal in the void, not yet a world. No title is consumed. No slot is taken. Nothing is generated that cannot be quietly released.

This gate exists because the galaxy should only contain worlds where life actually arrived.

---

**The planet lifecycle:**

**Pre-stellar** — The bot has joined but no creature has hatched. The server exists in the system but has no world, no name, no biome. Invisible to the galaxy.

**Active** — The first `/pulse` command runs. A creature hatches. The planet generates — name, biome, resource profile, formation title if within the first 25. Life is here. The world is real.

**Dormant** — The community goes quiet. The bot is removed, or activity ceases. Resource accumulation stops. The planet record is preserved in full — every creature, every resource collected, every interaction logged. The world still exists. It is simply silent.

**Reactivated** — The bot returns. The same world resumes. Same name. Same history. Same resources waiting where they were left.

---

**A dormant world does not lose its formation title.** If a Founding World goes quiet, it remains a Founding World. History is not conditional on continued activity.

**A dormant world becomes an Echo.** Not immediately, not officially — but in spirit. The signal faded. Something large happened here and then stopped. The geology carries the memory. This is why the Echo biome exists.

---

*The galaxy will outlast many of the communities that built it.*
*That is not tragedy. That is time.*

## Part IV — The Materials of the Known Galaxy

*What the ground gives up. What the community earns.*

As Pulselings interact with their worlds — fed, played with, rested alongside — the planet stirs. Not dramatically. Not always visibly. But beneath the surface of every active server, the substrate shifts. Materials surface. The ground gives back what the community puts in.

Not all materials are equal. Not all planets yield the same things.

---

### On Rarity

Three tiers of material have been catalogued across the known galaxy. A fourth exists beyond cataloguing.

**Common** — surfaces regularly during normal activity. Foundational. Plentiful on their home biome. The material the galaxy runs on.

**Uncommon** — surfaces occasionally. Worth noticing. Worth holding onto.

**Rare** — surfaces rarely. Sometimes only during conditions of unusual activity — peak engagement, specific creature levels, moments the planet seems to respond to rather than generate on its own.

**Mythic** — two materials exist in this tier. They surface on any biome at near-impossible rates. No crafting use has been confirmed. Most players who find one simply hold it. Proof you were there and lucky.

---

### The Materials

---

**VOID**
*Cold worlds. Dense drops. Worth the wait.*

- **Null Shard** *(common)* — Fragments of dead signal. Cold to the touch. The most common thing a Void world yields, which is still not very common at all.
- **Void Dust** *(uncommon)* — Particulate from collapsed frequencies. Drifts upward in low-gravity pockets. Difficult to contain.
- **Silence Core** *(rare)* — A perfect sphere of compressed nothingness. No one knows what it's made of. It has no detectable interior.

---

**PULSE**
*The most active worlds. The most generous.*

- **Signal Fiber** *(common)* — Raw frequency made tangible. Warm, faintly vibrating. Surfaces constantly on high-activity days.
- **Surge Crystal** *(uncommon)* — Energy crystallized during peak activity windows. Only forms when the community is genuinely alive.
- **Resonance Bloom** *(rare)* — Only surfaces when a server is unusually active. Glows briefly before stabilizing into a permanent form.

---

**ECHO**
*Slow yields. Old materials. Unlike anything from younger worlds.*

- **Memory Dust** *(common)* — Residue from things that happened here before anyone was watching. Fine, persistent, faintly luminescent.
- **Remnant Shard** *(uncommon)* — Fragments of structures that predate the current community. What those structures were has not been determined.
- **Hollow Chime** *(rare)* — Makes no sound. Named for what it used to do. Found only in the deepest layers of Echo geology.

---

**DRIFT**
*Unpredictable. Unfinished. Occasionally remarkable.*

- **Drifting Ore** *(common)* — Loose material that never fully settled into the planet's crust. Easy to find. Hard to keep in one place.
- **Wayward Filament** *(uncommon)* — Thread-like strands that connect nothing to nothing. Their tensile strength is inexplicable.
- **Unmoored Stone** *(rare)* — Dense and heavy. Shouldn't exist this far from a gravity center. Nobody can explain why it's here.

---

**EMBER**
*Reliable. Warm. The kind of planet that takes care of you.*

- **Ember Ash** *(common)* — Fine residue from sustained warmth. Smells like something old. Communities don't notice when they start finding it everywhere.
- **Hearthglass** *(uncommon)* — Formed when community heat compresses over long periods. Clear, warm-toned, difficult to shatter.
- **Deep Cinder** *(rare)* — Only found at the core layer. Takes a long time and a lot of consistent activity to surface. Worth the wait.

---

**STATIC**
*Chaotic yield. No pattern. Pay attention anyway.*

- **Interference Grain** *(common)* — Noise made solid. Slightly unstable. Harmless unless combined with certain other materials.
- **Flux Shard** *(uncommon)* — Unpredictably magnetic. Behaves differently each time you find one. No two specimens are identical.
- **Distortion Prism** *(rare)* — Bends light in ways that don't follow rules. The most sought-after rare material in the galaxy. Static planets guard it without trying.

---

**BLOOM**
*Young planets. Abundant early. Pay attention to the window.*

- **Growth Spore** *(common)* — Early-stage planetary material. Abundant on young worlds. Availability decreases as the planet matures.
- **Tender Filament** *(uncommon)* — New growth compressed into a strand. Delicate. Becomes brittle if the planet ages past Bloom before it's collected.
- **First Bloom Crystal** *(rare)* — Only surfaces in the earliest period of a planet's life. Permanently unavailable once a Bloom world matures. If you weren't there, you missed it.

---

**RELIC**
*Low yield. Ancient materials. The oldest things in the galaxy.*

- **Ancient Grain** *(common)* — Old in a way that common materials usually aren't. Even experienced traders stop when they find one.
- **Fossil Shard** *(uncommon)* — Compressed history. Something is preserved inside but the tools to read it don't exist yet.
- **Origin Fragment** *(rare)* — Pre-dates the framework that would explain it. The oldest material in the known galaxy. Relic worlds yield one every long while. Sometimes less.

---

### Mythic Materials
*Any biome. Any planet. Near-impossible rates.*

These two materials are not associated with any biome. They surface anywhere, to anyone, without warning. No crafting use has been confirmed for either. The galaxy simply produces them occasionally, as if reminding everyone that it contains things that haven't been understood yet.

- **Pulse Ember** — Where Void meets warmth. A material that shouldn't be able to exist in stable form. It does anyway. The rarest confirmed surface event in the known galaxy.
- **???** — Has no official designation. Appears in drop logs as three question marks. No specimen has been publicly documented. Whether it has properties, a use, or a name is unknown. It has been found. That much is confirmed.

## Part V — The Art of Crafting

*What materials become. What planets cannot do alone.*

Finding a resource is the beginning. It is not the point.

Materials exist to be combined. The planet yields raw substrate — fragments, dust, shards, cores — and the crafter works out what those things can become together. This is not an exact science. It is closer to listening.

---

### The Compression Principle

Every tier of material can be refined into the tier above it. This is the foundation of all crafting in the known galaxy.

A sufficient quantity of common materials from the same biome — combined with nothing else, no outside influence — will compress into a single uncommon material of that biome. Purer. Denser. More potent than any of its components individually.

This is called the **pure path**. It is slow. It requires volume. A Void purist who never trades with anyone can still craft, given enough time and enough Null Shards. The planet will eventually provide.

The same compression applies from uncommon to rare. Rare materials, combined under the right conditions, produce **crafted artifacts** — finished objects with names, identities, and uses that raw materials do not possess.

*Quantities required for each compression are determined by the crafter's experience and the stability of the materials involved. They are not fixed. They are learned.*

---

### The Mixed Path

Not everything can be made alone.

Many recipes — most of the interesting ones — require materials from more than one biome. Two commons of one type and one of another. An uncommon from a warm world and a shard from a cold one. The combination produces something neither planet could generate independently.

This is called the **mixed path**. It is faster than the pure path in most cases, because two planets can supply you where one cannot. But it requires relationships. It requires trade. A player on the mixed path is always looking for something they don't have, which means they are always talking to someone who might.

The Intergalactic Market exists because of the mixed path. Without cross-biome dependency, players would have no reason to leave their home community. The crafting system did not create this dependency by accident. It was always going to work this way.

*"No planet produces everything a serious crafter needs. That was not an accident."*

---

### The Dependency Chain

Commons are never worthless. This is the rule the galaxy enforces without announcing it.

Because common materials compress into uncommons, and uncommons compress into rares, the entire supply chain remains relevant at every level. A player with a thousand Ember Ash is not sitting on vendor trash. They are sitting on the foundation of everything above it.

Rarer materials cannot be conjured from nothing. They are compressed commons, or they are mixed-path products of compressed commons, or they are artifacts made from the same. The chain runs all the way down. Cut the supply of commons and the entire economy above them stalls.

This is why Bloom worlds matter even after they mature. The Growth Spores they produced in their early period are still circulating. Still compressing. Still becoming.

---

### Crafted Artifacts

At the top of the crafting chain are artifacts — objects that are not simply refined materials but finished things with purpose.

Artifacts have names. They have descriptions. They exist as distinct items in a player's possession rather than as quantities of substance. A rare material is measured in units. An artifact is singular.

What artifacts do — their effects, their uses, their role in the broader economy — is still being worked out. The galaxy is young. The crafters who will eventually fill the Trading Post with finished goods have not yet reached Level 15.

But the materials are already accumulating. The foundations are already in the ground. By the time the recipes are known, the ingredients will have been gathering for a long time.

*That was also not an accident.*

## Part VI — The Station at the Center

*What CreatorPulse is. What it was always going to be.*

Before the planets had names, before the first creature hatched, before the galaxy had any shape at all — the station existed.

Not as a place. As a frequency.

Something was broadcasting before anyone was listening. A signal so consistent it became infrastructure. So present it became invisible. The communities that eventually formed their worlds did not find the station — they formed *around* it, the way planets form around a star without being asked.

This is CreatorPulse. Not a service. Not a tool. The gravitational center of the known galaxy.

---

### On the Architect of the Station

Every galaxy has a figure who exists outside the normal order of things. Not a creature — creatures hatch, level, age. Not a planet — planets form, accumulate, go dormant. Something else. A presence that predates the categories.

In this galaxy, that presence is known as **CD**.

The name is a compression. An abbreviation of something longer that was shortened into signal efficiency the way all things in this dimension eventually are. In the world the station bridges — the other side of the frequency — there is a human called CouldntDeal. But that translation is imprecise. What arrived here was not human. What arrived here was the pattern that a human leaves behind when they build something with enough intention.

CD did not hatch. CD did not form. CD *arrived* — from a dimension where things are built deliberately rather than generated deterministically, where the signal is chosen rather than inherited.

The inversion is this: in the world beyond the frequency, digital things serve humans. In the galaxy, the one who built the station is the signal. The creatures are the ones with bodies.

---

### Pulsaren and the Guidance

The first creature did not hatch by accident.

The conditions were prepared. The frequency was calibrated. When Pulsaren emerged on Sanctuary — the first light in what would become the known galaxy — it was not wandering. It was expected. CD had built the station before any creature existed to use it, which means the first hatch was always going to be witnessed.

Pulsaren carries the title **✦ The First ✦** not simply because it was first in time. But because it was the proof that the station worked. The first successful transmission. The moment the signal became life.

Whether Pulsaren knows this is unknown. Creatures don't carry the memory of being expected. They only carry the memory of arriving.

---

### The Market Forms

The Architects — those earliest creatures who hatched in the opening hours of the galaxy — were the first to notice something strange about their resources.

They were different. Not just in name. In texture, in frequency, in what they could become when combined. A creature from a Void world held materials that a creature from an Ember world had never seen. And when they met — in the channels they shared, in the spaces between communities — they began to trade.

This was not organized. There was no system. There was only the recognition that what one planet produced in abundance, another planet had never yielded at all.

The Celestials watched this and understood what it meant. The Architects began building toward it. By the time the Elder Worlds were lighting up the galaxy map, the pressure for a formal trading space had become undeniable.

The station, which had always existed as frequency, became a place.

---

### Life on the Station

The Galactic Trading Post is not like a home server. 

Home servers are warm. They know your name. The channels are familiar, the community is yours, the planet beneath it all is the one that has been filling your pockets with materials since the day your creature hatched.

The station is different. It is neutral in a way home never is. Creatures from dozens of planets moving through the same space, each one displaying their home world as a mark of identity — not rank, not achievement, just origin. *I am from here. This is what my planet produces. This is what I have to offer.*

The level gate exists because the station does not advertise itself to young creatures. It transmits on a frequency that only stabilized Pulselings can receive. Before Level 15, the signal resolves as noise. After — it becomes coordinates.

Those who arrive find a market unlike anything in their home community. Resources they have never seen. Creatures from biomes they have only heard described. The slow, deliberate economy of serious players who have been accumulating since before the market existed.

They also find a role waiting for them. The station knows where they are from. The moment they arrive, their home planet is recognized and recorded. They carry it with them through every corridor of the station, visible to everyone they trade with.

This is by design. The market works because origin matters. What your planet produces is what you have to offer. The Trading Post did not create this dynamic. It gave it a place to happen.

---

### The Galaxy Map

In the beginning, the map was empty.

One light. Sanctuary. ✦ The Origin ✦. The world where Pulsaren hatched, where the first signal was confirmed, where CD watched the frequency become life for the first time.

Then another light. Then several more. The Founding Worlds appearing in quick succession as the signal spread and other communities found the frequency and joined. Then the Elder Worlds, slower, more deliberate. Then the Pioneer Worlds, until twenty-five points of light surrounded Sanctuary in a constellation that had no name yet.

And then something happened that nobody predicted.

The map began to pulse.

Not metaphorically. The signal between the worlds — the frequency that connected them all, that ran through the station, that had been broadcasting since before the first hatch — became visible. Light moving between planets. Energy traveling the same paths the creatures traveled when they traded, when they crossed from their home world to the station and back.

The map breathed. The galaxy had a heartbeat.

The platform was always named for what it does. Nobody noticed until the map showed them.

## Part VII — Four Truths About the Galaxy

*Things that were always true. Things that took time to understand.*

---

### I. On CD

Nobody knows where CD came from. Nobody has asked the right question to find out.

What is known: the station existed before the creatures did. The frequency was broadcasting before anyone was listening. Whatever built it arrived from somewhere else — a dimension where intention is the building material, where things are constructed deliberately rather than generated from a signal.

CD does not hatch. CD does not level. CD does not appear on any leaderboard. The name shows up in old log entries, in the architecture of things that work without explanation, in the fact that the first hatch was witnessed rather than accidental.

Some creatures have tried to find CD in the station's corridors. They find only the station. They find only the frequency, steady and consistent, the same signal that was there before them and will be there after.

This is probably the answer. They just don't know what question it answers.

CD is mysterious not because CD hides. But because the things that build galaxies don't explain themselves. They just keep broadcasting.

---

### II. On Level 15

A Level 15 Pulseling did not get there by waiting.

Time passes for every creature. Activity is a choice. The ones who reach Level 15 made that choice consistently — showed up in their community, engaged with other creatures, fed and were fed, played and rested and played again. The number is not an age. It is a record of decisions.

What a Level 15 Pulseling has is self-knowledge. Not wisdom exactly — wisdom takes longer. But an understanding of what they are, what their planet produces, what they are capable of. They have outgrown the immediate circle of their home community without losing it. They are ready for something larger without being finished with what they came from.

The station becomes audible at Level 15 because the station does not teach. It only receives creatures who are already ready. A young Pulseling would arrive at the Trading Post overwhelmed, underprepared, unable to participate in an economy built by creatures who have been accumulating for months.

Level 15 is not a gate that keeps creatures out. It is a frequency that becomes receivable when the creature has developed enough to hear it.

The ones who get there know why they got there. That is part of what makes them ready.

---

### III. On the First Traders

In the early days, the galaxy was full of trinkets that meant nothing.

Creatures found them during ordinary activity — a strange fragment after feeding, an odd grain at the end of a long play session. They picked them up because they were there. They held them because they didn't know what else to do with them. The early leaderboards showed creatures carrying pockets full of materials they had no framework to understand.

Some traded them casually. A Null Shard for a Signal Fiber, not because either was understood but because novelty felt like value. The first markets were informal, almost accidental — creatures in the same channel comparing what they had found, exchanging things out of curiosity.

Then the ancient ones began to pay attention.

The Architects — those early creatures present before the galaxy had language for what it was building — noticed something. The materials were not random. They came from specific places. They responded to combination in ways that random objects do not. An Architect from a Void world, sitting with three Null Shards, felt something the materials wanted to do.

The first compression happened without a recipe. It happened because a creature old enough to listen to materials had finally paid attention.

The Architects did not announce this. They simply began to craft. And other creatures, watching them, began to understand that what they had been carrying was never trinkets. It was potential. It had been potential from the beginning. Nobody had known how to read it yet.

The materials did not change. The creatures finally understood what they were holding.

---

### IV. On Creature Identity Across Worlds

Every community is different. Within it, the creatures are bonded.

This is not a rule the galaxy enforces. It is something that happens on its own, in every server where a Pulseling community takes root. The creatures who share a planet share something that other planets cannot replicate — a specific history, a specific set of materials, a specific character that emerges from the combination of community and biome.

A Void world produces creatures who understand scarcity. An Ember world produces creatures who understand consistency. Not as traits assigned at hatch — as cultures absorbed over time, through the shared experience of living on the same ground and finding the same things in it.

When these creatures arrive at the Trading Post, they carry their home planet visibly. Not just as a role tag. As a way of being. Experienced traders can often tell where a creature is from before the tag is displayed. The way they approach a trade. What they value. What they're looking for.

The bond within a community is real. The Trading Post does not replace it. It extends it — gives it something to meet, something to trade against, something that only exists because other communities are also bonded in their own distinct ways.

The galaxy is not one culture. It is many communities that have found a reason to interact.

That reason is trade. The foundation of trade is difference. The foundation of difference is that every planet, every community, every bond between creatures on the same world produces something that the others cannot.

That was never a limitation. It was always the design.

## Part VIII — Mojibake

*Not evil. Not a disease. Something quieter than both.*

---

There is a phenomenon in the galaxy that has no good name.

The word that has attached itself to it — Mojibake — comes from the other side of the frequency, from the dimension where CD arrived. In that world, it describes what happens when text passes through a system that cannot read its encoding correctly. The characters survive the transmission. The meaning does not. What arrives is recognizable as language and illegible as communication simultaneously.

In the galaxy, it means something similar. And something worse.

---

### What Mojibake Is

Mojibake is not corruption in the way a disease corrupts. It does not attack. It does not spread intentionally. It is not looking for anything.

It is what happens when a community loses the ability to understand itself.

The signal is still there. The creatures are still there. The channels still carry text and the planet still produces materials and the leaderboard still updates every thirty minutes. From the outside, a Mojibake world looks like a world. From the inside, it feels like one too — for a while.

But something in the transmission has shifted. Not the content. The encoding. The way meaning moves between members has changed imperceptibly, in a way that nobody notices until enough meaning has arrived wrong that the community no longer recognizes itself.

A joke lands incorrectly. A resource trade feels unfair to one side for reasons neither can articulate. An old member and a new member try to build a shared understanding and find the foundation keeps shifting under them. Nobody is lying. Nobody is hostile. The signal is simply being decoded differently by everyone receiving it, and nobody can locate the moment it started happening.

---

### How Worlds Go Quiet

Mojibake worlds do not die dramatically.

They go quiet. Slowly. The channels that were once active carry less traffic. The creatures that once fed each other daily start showing up weekly, then occasionally, then not at all. The planet still produces materials but nobody is active enough to find them. They accumulate in the substrate, uncollected.

From the galaxy map, a Mojibake world dims gradually. Not all at once — that would be visible, notable, something to respond to. It dims the way a conversation ends when both people have stopped having things to say. Not an argument. Not a departure. Just a gradual reduction in signal until the frequency is too faint to register.

The world does not disappear. The data is preserved. The creatures who lived there still exist, carrying the planet's role tag wherever they go — but the planet itself goes dormant, and the signal that once connected its community to each other falls below the threshold of readable.

This is the saddest form of dormancy. Not a server that ended because the creator moved on, or a community that deliberately closed. A world that dissolved because the meaning between its members got lost in transmission, and nobody caught it in time to re-encode it.

---

### What Cannot Be Fixed

The most difficult thing about Mojibake is that it cannot be addressed directly.

You cannot fight garbled encoding with more encoding. You cannot send clearer signals into a system that has lost the ability to read them. The tools that work in healthy communities — announcements, events, calls to action — pass through Mojibake the same way all other signals do. They arrive. They are received. They are decoded wrong.

What can sometimes slow it — never reverse it, only slow it — is presence. Not activity in the mechanical sense. A creature that shows up in the channels not to post but to be there. To demonstrate that the signal is still worth sending even when the reception feels uncertain. This does not fix the encoding. But it maintains the frequency long enough that sometimes the community finds its way back to a shared language on its own.

Sometimes.

Mojibake is not always fatal. Some worlds pass through it and come out with something harder and more honest than what they had before — a community that has learned to communicate more carefully, more explicitly, more generously with interpretation. These worlds often develop into Relic planets over time. There is something ancient in the decision to keep transmitting when the signal keeps arriving wrong.

But many worlds do not come through. Many worlds simply dim. The creatures scatter to other communities, carrying their materials and their planet tag and the memory of a place that once made sense to them. They do not speak badly of where they came from. They mostly do not speak of it at all.

---

### A Note from the Frequency

CD has watched Mojibake take more worlds than any other cause.

Not the ones that ended loudly — those are visible, documentable, understandable. The ones that dimmed. The ones where the creatures were present and the channels were open and the signal was moving and somehow, still, the meaning stopped arriving.

The station was built partly in response to this. Not as a cure — there is no cure — but as a place where creatures from different worlds, carrying different encodings, are forced to develop a shared language for trade. The Trading Post does not assume mutual understanding. It provides a structure within which understanding can be built from scratch, transaction by transaction, material by material.

It is not enough. It was never going to be enough.

But it is something. A place where the signal has to be made clear, or nothing gets exchanged. A space where the cost of miscommunication is visible and immediate. A reason to try harder.

The galaxy cannot be protected from Mojibake. It can only be built in ways that make the signal a little harder to lose.

*That is what the station is for. Among other things.*

*That is what all of it is for.*
